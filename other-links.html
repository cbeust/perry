Dear All
 
I hope I'm not teaching grandmother to suck eggs here <smile> but I thougt I'd post some things I have discovered (usually by accident) concerning out translating hobby.  many of the folk here seem to have bought and attempt to translate the actual novels.  I'm not in that position sadly - I used to have over 1200 of the german editions but they were destroyed in a fire at the storage utility I used. 
 
I have used personal translating packages such as Power Translator and Universal Translator, but while they have the advantages ofhaving personal modifiable dictionaries I find the on-line versions much faster and easier (provided the sourse text is modified slightly first).
 
Everyone knows about and has had agood laugh with Babelfish (http://world.altavista.com/sites/gben/pos/babelfish/trns) but SysTrans have improved the engine significantly and its works pretty well for german and French.  My prefered option is Prompt Reverso's engine available at http://translation2.paralink.com/ and http://www.translate.ru/eng/ as they handle source of any length whereas babelfish bows aout after a few hundered words.  Both these options allow you to either enter text or a URL to a page and then allow subsequent following o flinks, but in my experience it proves more reliable to right click the link and open in a new browser wndow .  Another great engine, but one which does not allow link-following is http://www.freetranslation.com/web.htm.  This can provide a very and reliable translation.  I usually have open the dictionary at http://translator.dictionary.com/text.html which seems extensive and I ind useful for those single words or compound nouns the engines couldn't cope with.  I tend to hit a translation with two engines and arrange have the windows open side by side and block copy the sentence from eith that makes the most obvious sense.
 
Which brings me nicely to summaries. 
ENGLISH: Cedric's page you all must know is at http://www.beust.com/cedric/perry and has summaries of varying length from novels 200 - 299, 300 - 370 or so, (current in France), the 650 - 700, 700 - 800, and 800 to about 810 which he is updating as he reads.   He also has 1900 - 1999 and 2000 - 2029 last time I looked. My site will have 100 - 149, 150 - 200, 1400 - 1499 (eventually extending through to 1799 as I have full machine translations of all books higher than 1428).  I may continue the Tolkander (1800 - 1875) cycle of Thoregon but that depends on my role in the fan translation effort.
FRENCH: There are great cycle summaries at http://www.ensg.ign.fr/Pages_Perso/Pages_de_J_Y_PRIOU/PR_Cycles_VO/PR_CyclesVO.html as well as an alomost complete book by book account o fthe French series (currently up to 300, for example his individual summaries for the Second empire (150 - 199 in German) are at http://www.ensg.ign.fr/Pages_Perso/Pages_de_J_Y_PRIOU/PR_Cycles_FR/PR_CycleFR_IV.html.  He has also thoughfully added summaries of the edition the french publishes did not translate (like the several Ace did not at first), so careful with tose links.  Another key site is http://membres.lycos.fr/jeddak/cadrerho.htm who also has summaries (resumees) for th efirst 100 and from 300 to French current), and from 2100 to current in Germany.  His method though is tricky for us machine translators, he ha disabled right-click copy, which remains intact even after the translation engine has done its job.  No fear, save the translate page as a html doc and open with a web editor like FrontPage, or a word processor.  Alternatively save as text.
GERMANY: A little wheat and plenty of chaff about here.  The wheat is mainly at http://www.prchronik.net, part of the might proc.org fan organisation that powers Dorgon.  Thomas Rabenstein has very very detaile summaires from 1900 - to current, as well as very detailed cycle summaries which actually breal down into a blow by blow account of the hefte.  The on-line magazine but to avoid ploughing though and finding the reviews in the individual editions of the magazine, point the web translator here http://www.flash-zine.de/allgem/pr_list.htm.  Another good source (pun intended) is the matter source at http://www.materiequell e.de, he has summries from the 1 - 55 and 1428 up (some are not linked but can be accessedby adjust the number in the address bar.  Good detail in the cycle summaries and lexicon as these are derived from the official books published in germany.  Summaries of varying length and detail for scratchy sections o fthe series are also at http://www.hjp.at/pr/zusammenfassungen/hefte.html.
 
Combining translators and the links to prodcue "smart links":  This is an example of a smart link which can be saved and take you straight to the page via the translation engine.  This can then be saved as a shortcut on your desk top or a folder. http://world.altavista.com/sites/gben/pos/babelfish/urltrurl?url=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.pr-materiequelle.de%2Fbegriffe%2Fhefte%2Fhefte.htm&lp=de_en&tt=url&urltext=
To generate one, go to the translation engine of choice and enter the url you wish to transate.  Let it do the business and then right click on the resulting page and save as..shortcut to desktop.  One the shortcut has been made, it can be copied or moved to wherever you store them.
 
I hope this has been useful and points you in the direction of some good summaries.
